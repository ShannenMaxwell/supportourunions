Union Basics and Benefits

Since labor unions were legalized in 1842, they have swelled and receded in terms of popularity and influence. From the heyday of the Knights of Labor in the 1880s and the American Federation of Labor in the early 20th century, to New Deal legislation that gave unions new protections, the backlash and decline in union membership in the second half of the 20th century, to the recent election of an unabashedly pro-union president, the story of unions is a story of America.

so what do unions actually do for their members?

What are unions bargaining for?
Infogram

Unions give workers a seat at the table and a say in decisionmaking. This seat at the table also translates to tangible outcomes in pay, with unionized workers are paid over $100 more a week on average than their non-unionized peers.

Unions on the Decline

Union membership has been declining in the United States for over 50 years. In 1964, nearly 30% of all American workers were in a union. Today only about 10% of workers are unionized. As illustrated below, the most drastic declines have been experienced by states in the industrial Midwest. (Source: Hirsch, Macpherson, Vroman. 2021. Monthly Labor Review. Union % of total non-agricultural employment)

Unions in certain sectors of the economy have been hit especially hard. Manufacturing and construction have experienced the sharpest declines in union membership over the past 5 decades, falling by about 30 percentage points.

The decline of union membership can be exemplified through the much weaker negotiating position workers have held in recent decades. While it was not uncommon for the U.S. to experience well over 300 major strikes each year prior to 1980, in recent decades the U.S. generally only experiences about 20 major strikes involving at least 1000 workers.

Implications Today

This suppression of worker wages has contributed to a widening wage gap between working class and upper class Americans. As shown below, unionization and income inequality have been closely related since the 1970s.

While this cannot prove a causal relationship on its own, several academic studies have. These studies have found that declining unionization alone has directly accounted for a fifth to a third of all new income inequality experienced since the 1970s. Other contributing factors to inequality include globalization, technology, and greater returns to education.

Although the decline in unionization has been a decades-long trend, a window of opportunity may be opening to reverse this. In the past ten years, attitudes towards labor unions have been steadily trending upward, which may be able to translate into political support. Approval of labor unions is now at the same level as it was in the early 1960s–when the rate of unionization was three times higher.

2021 has been marked by a rise in workers feeling empowered to demand more from their employers. Major strikes, including those at John Deere and Kellogg’s, may convince workers across the country of the power and benefits that labor unions provide.
